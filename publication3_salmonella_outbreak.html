Salmonella Outbreak Linked to Frozen Sprouted Beans

Published:June 1, 2024 | Dr. Chari| Source: Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC)

Overview and Key Content of the Outbreak Alert

This public health alert, issued by the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC) on July 17, 2025, details a multistate outbreak of *Salmonella* Anatum infections.[22, 23] The investigation has definitively linked this outbreak to specific products: recalled Deep brand frozen sprouted moong (mung) beans and frozen sprouted moth (mat) beans.[22, 23]

The alert provides crucial information regarding the scope of the outbreak, including the number of individuals affected and hospitalizations, alongside details of the product recall. It also outlines immediate, actionable steps for both consumers and businesses to mitigate further risk, and offers general information about *Salmonella* infections.[22, 23]

Health Issues Associated with Salmonella Infection and Affected Population

*Salmonella* infection, known as salmonellosis, is a common bacterial disease that primarily targets the intestinal tract.[24] While it typically manifests as gastroenteritis (commonly referred to as food poisoning), it can also progress to more severe systemic infections, such as enteric fevers (e.g., typhoid fever) or septicemia, which can be life-threatening.[25]

Complications, though not always present, can be serious. If the infection spreads beyond the intestines into the bloodstream (bacteremia), it can lead to life-threatening conditions by infecting various tissues throughout the body. These include the urinary system (resulting in urinary tract infections), the membranes surrounding the brain and spinal cord (meningitis), the lining of the heart or its valves (endocarditis), bones and bone marrow (osteomyelitis), or the lining of blood vessels.[24] Severe dehydration can also occur if fluid loss from diarrhea is not adequately replenished.[24] A smaller proportion of individuals may develop reactive arthritis (also known as Reiter's syndrome), characterized by joint pain, eye irritation, and painful urination, which can persist for months or even years.[24]

Affected Population (as of July 16, 2025):

  • Cases: 11 individuals infected with the outbreak strain of *Salmonella* Anatum have been reported across 10 states.[23]
  • Onset Dates: Illnesses started on dates ranging from October 22, 2024, to June 24, 2025.[23]
  • Outcomes: Among the 9 reported cases for which information was available, 4 individuals required hospitalization. No fatalities have been reported.[23]
  • Demographics: Median age was 43 years (range: <1 to 78 years). 55% female, 45% male. All (100%) were identified as Asian and non-Hispanic.[23]
  • Food Consumption and Shopping Habits: All 6 interviewed individuals (100%) had shopped at Indian (South Asian) grocery stores and consumed Indian cuisines. 4 of these 6 (67%) reported eating or likely eating sprouted beans.[23]
  • Underreporting: The actual number of affected individuals is likely much higher, as many recover without seeking medical care or testing. There is also typically a 3-4 week delay in reporting recent illnesses.[23]

Certain populations are at a significantly higher risk for severe illness and complications from *Salmonella* infection, including infants, young children, older adults (aged 65 and above), pregnant women, transplant recipients, and individuals with weakened immune systems due to conditions such as HIV/AIDS, sickle cell disease, malaria, or immunosuppressive medications.[24]

Symptoms of Salmonella Infection

The symptoms of *Salmonella* infection can vary but commonly include:

Symptom Category Specific Symptoms Onset/Duration Notes When to Seek Medical Attention
Common Gastrointestinal Diarrhea (often watery, sometimes with blood or mucus), severe stomach/abdominal cramps, nausea, vomiting, loss of appetite.[26] Typically begin 6 hours to 6 days after exposure. Usually last 4 to 7 days. Diarrhea may persist up to 10 days; bowel habits may take months to normalize.[26] Diarrhea or vomiting lasting more than 2 days.[26]
Other Common Symptoms Fever (up to 102°F or 38-39°C), headache, chills.[25, 26] Fever higher than 102°F.[26]
Severe/Complication Signs Dehydration signs (urinating less, dark urine, dry mouth/tongue, sunken eyes, no tears, increased tiredness, irritability, confusion). Bloody stool or pee. Reactive arthritis (joint pain, eye irritation, painful urination).[24, 26] Dehydration can happen quickly, especially in young children. Reactive arthritis is most common in people aged 15-35 years.[26] Any signs of dehydration. Bloody stool or pee. If the affected person is an infant, young child, older adult, or has a weakened immune system.[24, 26]

Prevention and Food Safety Tips

Immediate Action for Recalled Products:

  • Individuals possessing any recalled Deep brand frozen sprouted moong or moth (mat) beans must not consume, sell, or serve them. These products should be immediately discarded or returned to the point of purchase.[22, 23]
  • Any items or surfaces that may have come into contact with the recalled products should be thoroughly washed using hot soapy water or a dishwasher.[22] Businesses are advised to adhere to the U.S. Food and Drug Administration's (FDA) guidelines for safe handling and cleaning.[22]

General Sprout Safety:

  • Avoid Raw/Undercooked Sprouts: A primary recommendation is to avoid consuming raw or undercooked sprouts, particularly for vulnerable populations such as children, older adults, pregnant women, and immunocompromised individuals. Thorough cooking effectively eliminates most harmful bacteria.[27]
  • Shopping Practices: When purchasing sprouts, ensure they are refrigerated and appear crisp. Avoid sprouts that look dark or emit a musty odor. Keep sprouts separate from raw meat, poultry, and seafood in the grocery cart to prevent cross-contamination.[27]
  • Chilling and Storage: Sprouts should be refrigerated immediately upon returning home. Non-prepackaged sprouts should be consumed within a few days. Any sprouts past their "best before" date or those that have lost crispness, darkened, or smell musty should be discarded.[27] Raw seed sprouts must be maintained under refrigeration at a temperature not exceeding 41°F (5°C) and stored in a clean, dry environment, away from automatic misters.[28] Packaged sprouts must be clearly labeled "Perishable- keep refrigerated".[28]
  • Cleaning and Hygiene: Hands should be washed thoroughly with warm water and soap for at least 20 seconds before and after handling sprouts. All utensils, countertops, and cutting boards that come into contact with sprouts must also be thoroughly cleaned.[27]
  • Source of Contamination: Raw seed sprouts are classified as a potentially hazardous food due to their propensity to support the rapid growth of infectious microorganisms like *Salmonella* and *E. coli* O157:H7. The seed itself is frequently identified as the initial source of contamination in sprout-related outbreaks.[28]
